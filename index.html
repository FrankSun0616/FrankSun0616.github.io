About Me

I am currently a PhD student in Physics at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology. My primary interests are in experimental high energy physics and in the computational methods required for the analysis of large and complex scientific datasets.

Before coming to HKUST, I completed an M.S. in Physics at Brown University and a B.A. in Physics and Mathematics at Macalester College. Across these stages of training, I have been interested in questions that sit at the intersection of physics, data analysis, and scientific computation.

My publications reflect this range of interests, including work in collider physics, machine learning for physics analysis, redshift survey science, stellar nucleosynthesis, and cosmology.

Research

Research Interests and Experience

My research has developed across several related areas in particle physics and astrophysics.

Experimental particle physics at HKUST

My doctoral work at HKUST is in experimental high energy physics. My current interests include analysis in the CERN environment, with particular emphasis on ATLAS-related topics and Higgs physics.

HKUST ATLAS Higgs physics

Machine learning for CMS

During my master's study at Brown University, I worked on machine learning methods for CMS jet calibration, with a focus on collider reconstruction and data analysis.

Brown CMS ML

Tianlai survey science

I am also a co-author of the Tianlai-WIYN North Celestial Cap Redshift Survey, which concerns low-redshift galaxy observations and their use in support of 21 cm intensity mapping.

Tianlai Redshift survey astro-ph

Stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis

My work in stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis has examined the role of initial composition in massive star evolution and chemical enrichment.

ApJ nucleosynthesis computational astrophysics

Cosmology with Gauss-Bonnet torsion

I have also contributed to work in theoretical cosmology, including a study of renormalizable cosmology based on Gauss-Bonnet theory with torsion.

gr-qc Gauss-Bonnet torsion
